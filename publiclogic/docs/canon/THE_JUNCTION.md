## The honest claim

Do **not** claim nobody has built the parts. They plainly exist:

- **Records management** preserves the record — metadata, fixity, lifecycle, transferability.
- **Policy-as-code** (e.g. Open Policy Agent) makes explicit allow/deny decisions from
  machine-readable rules.
- **Zero-trust architecture** continuously evaluates access instead of assuming authority from
  location or org membership.
- **Verifiable credentials** make claims portable, tamper-evident, independently verifiable.
- **Digital public infrastructure** increasingly calls for shared, interoperable building blocks
  over isolated agency systems.

**Each field stops one layer too early.** Records systems prove a file was preserved, not that
the institution had *authority* to take the action the file represents. Authorization systems
decide whether a request may proceed, but rarely leave a durable, portable record explaining the
decision. Workflow software moves work but treats governance as configuration. Form builders
capture answers but do not model authority. Signature systems prove *who signed*, not whether
that person's role, process, source data, and governing covenant made the action *legitimate*.

So the credible claim is not "nobody made verifiable records or governed workflows." It is:

> **The market built the components separately. PuddleJumper assembles them into a governed-work
> runtime where authorization, execution, evidence, portability, and continuing assurance are one
> system.**

That is both more defensible and more impressive than a novelty claim: the opportunity exists
not because everyone else was stupid, but because the thing sits *between* established
categories.

## The compiled object

Most gov-tech inherited the paper metaphor: put the form online, route it, attach a signature,
store the PDF, call it transformation. PuddleJumper asks a different question:

> **What would software look like if the primary compiled object were not the document or the
> workflow, but the institution's legitimate authority to act?**

And the runtime joins into **one execution chain** what the separate fields keep apart:

> **What was allowed, under whose authority, using which institutional knowledge, within what
> boundary, producing what evidence — and what remains provable afterward?**

Allowed · authority · knowledge · boundary · evidence · durable proof. One chain. That junction
is the product.

## Two assurances, not one

The hard object is not the completed form. It is the relationship among: the **covenant version**
effective at execution; the **actor's authority** at that moment; the **source data** available
within that authorization region; later **revocation or supersession**; the **immutable historical
event**; and the institution's **current operating state**.

A static seal proves bytes have not changed. It cannot, by itself, answer every later question
about continuing authority, superseded rules, or whether new work may inherit from an old record.
So the runtime holds two distinct things — and keeping them distinct is the point:

- **Portable historical proof** — the customer keeps independently provable evidence of what was
  done, under what authority, forever. This does not depend on us.
- **Continuous runtime assurance** — current covenants, active authorization, new seals,
  validation, inheritance, continuity. This is the recurring capacity they pay for.

Preservation is separated from continuing institutional judgment.

## Why the seat was empty

1. **The buyer asks for the visible object.** Procurement starts with "we need permitting
   software / online forms / records management / workflow automation" — never "we need an
   institutional authorization and evidentiary runtime." Vendors optimize for the named category
   and demo the visible object. The governed substrate is hard to put in an RFP and hard to show
   in five minutes. *The market kept buying razors, so companies kept making razors.*
2. **The architecture crosses organizational ownership.** Doing it right combines administrative
   law and delegated authority, records practice, identity/access, provenance, workflow
   execution, tenant isolation, policy versioning, usable forms, portable evidence, and
   institutional change over time — functions that live in different departments, vendors, fields,
   and budget lines. **The integration problem is institutional before it is technical.**
3. **Portability contradicts conventional software economics.** Lock-in is manufactured by holding
   the database, proprietary formats, and history hostage. This model does the opposite: the
   customer keeps independently provable evidence and pays for *continuing governed capacity*.
   Renewal must be earned through ongoing value, not hostage value — a harder company to design,
   and a reason incumbents had no incentive to build it.
4. **"Correct over time" is brutally harder than "correct now."** A seal proves bytes are
   unchanged; it does not track superseded covenants, revoked authority, or valid inheritance.
   Most systems solved *now* and stopped.
5. **Gov-tech was digitizing documents, not compiling governance.** Even modern interoperability
   work focuses on shared identity, payments, data exchange, reusable components — essential, but
   it does not encode the institution's authority structure into every action.
6. **This frame was reached through operations, not abstraction.** A technologist sees forms,
   APIs, permissions, databases. A records professional sees retention and provenance. A lawyer
   sees authority and validity. A municipal operator sees turnover, undocumented exceptions,
   institutional memory, and the gap between what policy says and what happens. Meeting all of
   them *as one failure pattern* is why the frame is coherent — it was derived from the point
   where the separate systems fail **together.**

## The line

The opportunity exists because the thing sits between established categories, contradicts
conventional lock-in economics, and becomes obvious only when you treat **institutional
continuity** — not forms, not automation, not records — as the product.
