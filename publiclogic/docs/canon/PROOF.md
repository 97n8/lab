# PROOF

The three statements the rest of the canon hangs off. Everything else — schema, templates, work packs, lanes, dashboards, positioning — is an elaboration of these.

## The three foundational principles

1. **CaseSpace is the only top-level primitive** — the one thing that opens, moves, and seals. Owner / Subject / Resource / Window are its *parts*, not peers; the OPS modules are presets over those parts.
2. **PuddleJumper owns the two guarantees: Entry (provenance) and Repository (immutability).**
3. **The middle is content-agnostic but governance-aware.**

> **The moat is #2 and #3 — the load-bearing pair.** Not a feature a competitor lacks; a *stance* they cannot take while staying what they are. A workflow tool that owns only the bookends stops being a workflow tool; a system of record that refuses the middle stops being a system of record.

## What the bookends guarantee

- **Entry Surface** — work becomes a CaseSpace (received → logged). Guarantee: **provenance**.
- **Repository** — work becomes a sealed, append-only record. Guarantee: **immutability**. Typed, not a state chain: PRR ends at `closed` (**state**) → ARCHIEVE seals the leaf (**operation**) → the parent renders it as rollup (**view**).

The middle — Gmail, Drive, SharePoint, Munis, Tyler, OpenGov, Polimorphic, Airbnb, Hospitable, spreadsheets, paper, people — belongs to the operator. PJ governs the **movement** of the CaseSpace, not the software. Every transition is wrapped by CAL (authorized · valid · complete-enough-to-advance · eligible-to-seal). This is what the seal covers: the case record and its transition history — never the operator's CAMA, PMS, or ERP state.

## The category

**Governed transitions between two fixed contracts — provenance at entry, immutability at exit.** Not workflow. Not a system of record. Not an integration platform.
